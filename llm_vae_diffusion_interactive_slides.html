Part 1 — LLMs
What is a Large Language Model?
A very well-read autocomplete — trained on almost all human writing
Core idea
Next-word prediction
The entire secret of an LLM is learning to predict what word comes next. Given "The cat sat on the ___", it predicts "mat". Repeat billions of times and it learns grammar, facts, reasoning — everything.
Scale
Why "large"?
GPT-3 has 175 billion parameters — like 175 billion tiny dials, all tuned during training. A child hears ~100 million words by age 10. GPT-3 trained on 500 billion words.
Surprising result
Emergent abilities
Nobody programmed LLMs to pass the bar exam or write code. These abilities emerged from scale — just like a child who reads enough books eventually starts writing stories. Even the researchers at OpenAI were surprised.
📄 Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models
Kaplan et al., OpenAI · 2020 · arXiv:2001.08361
This paper showed that LLM performance improves predictably as you add more data, compute, and parameters — following a "power law". It became the blueprint for why companies kept making models bigger. A key read for understanding why GPT-4 is so much better than GPT-2.
Part 1 — LLMs
Tokens — the alphabet of an LLM
AI does not read letters or whole words — it reads "chunks" called tokens
What is a token?
A token is a chunk of text — sometimes a whole word, sometimes part of one. "unbelievable" might be split into "un", "believ", "able". The model never sees letters; it works with these chunks and turns each into a list of numbers (called a vector or embedding) that captures its meaning.
Click a token to highlight it
Embeddings
Turning words into numbers
Each token becomes a long list of numbers that captures meaning. "King" and "Queen" land close together. "Paris" and "France" share a relationship similar to "Berlin" and "Germany". The model reasons using these number-lists, not letters.
Part 1 — LLMs
The Transformer — attention is everything
The engine inside ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and almost every modern AI
What is attention?
When reading "The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big" — your brain instantly knows "it" refers to the trophy. Attention is how the model does the same thing: it weighs how much each word should "pay attention to" every other word in the sentence.
Click a word to see what it attends to (line thickness = attention strength)
📄 Attention Is All You Need — Vaswani et al., Google Brain · 2017 · arXiv:1706.03762
One of the most cited papers in AI history — 90,000+ citations
This paper introduced the Transformer architecture: instead of reading text left-to-right (like older models), it reads ALL words at once and uses "self-attention" to figure out relationships. It was originally designed to translate languages, but turned out to be the foundation of every major LLM. The name is a Beatles reference — "All You Need Is Love" → "Attention Is All You Need".
Part 1 — LLMs
How an LLM is trained
From raw internet text to a helpful assistant — in three stages
Stage 1
Pre-training
Read the internet
Stage 2
Fine-tuning
Learn to follow instructions
Stage 3
RLHF
Human feedback shapes behavior
Stage 1 — Pre-training
The model is shown billions of sentences and must predict the next word each time. It slowly adjusts its 175 billion dials (parameters) to get better. This is purely self-supervised — no humans involved.
Stage 2 — Fine-tuning
Without fine-tuning, if you ask "What is your first name?" the model might answer "What is your last name?" — because it just completes text. Fine-tuning teaches it that questions expect answers.
Stage 3 — RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)
Human raters compare two model answers and pick the better one. The model learns to generate responses humans prefer — more helpful, safer, more honest. This is what turns a raw model into ChatGPT or Claude.
📄 Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback
Ouyang et al., OpenAI (InstructGPT) · 2022 · arXiv:2203.02155
The paper that introduced RLHF to language models. It showed that a smaller model trained with human feedback outperformed a much larger model on helpfulness tasks. This became the foundation of ChatGPT.
Part 1 — LLMs
What LLMs can't do — yet
Understanding the limits keeps us from being fooled
Hallucinations
LLMs generate the most statistically likely next word — they don't "look things up". So they can confidently state false facts. Always verify important information from LLM outputs.
No real understanding
Some researchers argue LLMs are "remixing and recombining existing writing" — a phenomenon called the stochastic parrot. Others disagree. This debate is still open.
Bias in, bias out
If the training data contains biases, the model reflects them. This is why AI fairness and governance are active research areas.
Energy cost
Training GPT-4 reportedly used as much electricity as thousands of homes. Running these models at scale has a real environmental footprint.
📄 On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?
Bender, Gebru et al. · FAccT 2021
A controversial and influential critique of large language models, arguing that they match patterns without understanding meaning. It sparked debate about the ethics, environmental cost, and societal risks of ever-larger models. Required reading for anyone thinking critically about AI.
Part 2 — VAEs
Variational Autoencoders — the concept compressor
How AI learns to squish images into ideas — and create new ones
The core idea: compress, then create
Imagine learning to describe every face using just 10 numbers: how round it is, how light the skin is, whether it's smiling, etc. A VAE automatically learns these "hidden dimensions" (called the latent space) from data — without being told what the dimensions mean. Then it can generate new faces by picking new sets of numbers.

Key difference from regular autoencoders
A regular autoencoder maps each image to a single point. A VAE maps it to a range of possibilities (a probability distribution). This allows smooth, creative generation — not just copying.
Why this matters
VAEs are the "image compressor" inside Stable Diffusion. DALL-E uses a discrete version. They are also used in drug discovery, anomaly detection, and music generation.
📄 Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes
Kingma & Welling · NeurIPS 2013 · arXiv:1312.6114
The paper that invented VAEs. Kingma and Welling showed how to train a neural network that learns a smooth, structured "concept space" from data. The key trick — the reparameterization trick — allowed gradients to flow through random sampling, which had previously been impossible. A foundational paper in generative AI.
Part 3 — Stable Diffusion
Painting from noise
How AI creates images — by slowly removing randomness
The core intuition
Imagine someone shows you a masterpiece painting, then slowly adds random paint splatters until it looks like pure noise. A diffusion model learns to run that process backwards — starting from noise and gradually cleaning it up, guided by a text prompt.

Three components
How Stable Diffusion works
VAE — compresses the image to latent space to save compute. U-Net — the denoising brain, predicts and removes noise step by step. Text encoder — translates your prompt into numbers that guide the denoising direction.
Latent diffusion
Why "latent"?
Earlier diffusion models worked on full-resolution pixel grids — slow and memory-hungry. Stable Diffusion runs the denoising in a compressed latent space (using the VAE), then decodes to pixels only at the end. Much faster.
📄 High-Resolution Image Synthesis with Latent Diffusion Models
Rombach, Blattmann et al. · CVPR 2022 · arXiv:2112.10752
The paper behind Stable Diffusion. The key innovation was moving the denoising process into a compressed latent space (using a VAE), making high-resolution image generation feasible on consumer hardware. The model was publicly released, enabling a wave of open-source AI art tools.
Synthesis
How it all connects
LLMs, VAEs, and Diffusion Models are not separate — they talk to each other
DALL-E and Stable Diffusion
These systems combine a text encoder (from LLM research) with a VAE (to compress images) and a diffusion model (to generate them). The text encoder translates your words into a direction the denoiser follows.
Multimodal AI
Systems like GPT-4o can see images, hear audio, and read text — all at once. They combine LLM-style transformers with encoders for each modality. The shared idea: everything becomes tokens, and attention connects them.
What comes next
Researchers are building models that can reason across text, images, video, and action — AI agents that can browse the web, write code, and control software. The core ideas (prediction, attention, compression, denoising) remain the same.
📄 An Image is Worth 16×16 Words — Vision Transformer (ViT)
Dosovitskiy et al., Google Brain · ICLR 2021 · arXiv:2010.11929
This paper showed that the Transformer — originally designed for text — works remarkably well on images too, by splitting an image into a grid of small patches ("tokens"). It bridged the worlds of language AI and vision AI, and led directly to multimodal models.
📄 CLIP — Learning Transferable Visual Models from Natural Language Supervision
Radford et al., OpenAI · 2021 · arXiv:2103.00020
CLIP trained a model to match images and text descriptions together. It became the "text encoder" inside Stable Diffusion and DALL-E, allowing those systems to understand what a prompt means visually. The paper demonstrated that image and language understanding can be unified.
Further research
Key papers — annotated
A reading list for students who want to go further
📄 Attention Is All You Need
Vaswani et al. · Google Brain · NeurIPS 2017 · arXiv:1706.03762
Why it matters: Invented the Transformer — the engine inside every major LLM. Replaced old sequential models with parallel "attention" over all words at once. Key idea: Queries, Keys, and Values — attention as a smart database lookup. Impact: 90,000+ citations. Every LLM today descends from this paper.
📄 Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes
Kingma & Welling · NeurIPS 2013 · arXiv:1312.6114
Why it matters: Invented VAEs — generative models that learn smooth concept spaces. Key idea: The reparameterization trick — sampling randomness in a way that still allows the model to learn. Impact: Foundation of Stable Diffusion's image compression, DALL-E's discrete VAE, and countless generative models.
📄 Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)
Ho, Jain, Abbeel · NeurIPS 2020 · arXiv:2006.11239
Why it matters: The paper that made diffusion models practical for image generation. Key idea: Train a model to predict and remove noise at each step of a fixed "noising schedule". Impact: The direct ancestor of Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 2, and Midjourney.
📄 High-Resolution Image Synthesis with Latent Diffusion Models
Rombach et al. · CVPR 2022 · arXiv:2112.10752
Why it matters: The paper behind Stable Diffusion. Key idea: Run denoising inside a compressed VAE latent space — 8× smaller than pixels — making HD image generation affordable. Impact: First open-source high-quality text-to-image model, enabling an entire ecosystem of tools.
📄 Training language models to follow instructions (InstructGPT)
Ouyang et al. · OpenAI · 2022 · arXiv:2203.02155
Why it matters: The paper behind ChatGPT's helpfulness. Key idea: RLHF — have humans rank responses, train the model to prefer human-preferred outputs. Impact: Showed that a smaller model with better training beats a much bigger raw model.
