Continuous Bag of Words — Mikolov et al., 2013
CBOW: use the surrounding words to predict the missing word
Imagine covering a word in a sentence with your thumb. Can you guess it from the words around it? That is exactly what CBOW trains the model to do — billions of times. In doing so, the model learns that words appearing in similar contexts must mean similar things.

How the network is structured
Strengths
Faster to train. Works well with frequent words. Smoother, more averaged representations. Good for large corpora where speed matters.
Weaknesses
Averaging the context vectors loses word order. Less accurate for rare words — infrequent words get overwhelmed by common ones in the average.
Skip-gram — Mikolov et al., 2013 (same paper, opposite direction)
Skip-gram: use one word to predict all the words around it
Skip-gram flips CBOW around. Given one target word, the model must predict each of the surrounding context words individually. It "skips" across the window, predicting each neighbor — hence the name. This forces the model to build a richer representation of each word.

How the network is structured
Strengths
Excellent for rare words — each rare word gets many training signals. Captures richer, more nuanced word relationships. State of the art for small-to-medium corpora.
Weaknesses
Slower to train — each position generates multiple training examples. Computationally more expensive. Needs negative sampling tricks to be practical at scale.
Key trick: Negative Sampling
Making skip-gram fast enough to train
Predicting the correct word from 50,000 options is very slow. Negative sampling cheats: instead of updating all 50,000 output weights, the model randomly picks 5–20 "wrong" words (negatives) and only updates those plus the correct one. This makes training ~1000x faster with nearly the same result quality.
CBOW vs Skip-gram — the same paper, two opposite ideas
Both algorithms were introduced in the same 2013 Word2Vec paper by Mikolov et al. They share the same embedding table and training loop — only the direction of prediction is reversed. Think of it as asking "given the context, what is the word?" (CBOW) vs "given the word, what is the context?" (Skip-gram).
CBOW
Best for
Large corpora, frequent words, when training speed is the priority. Used in older production pipelines.
Skip-gram
Best for
Smaller corpora, rare or technical vocabulary, when word quality is the priority. Default choice for most Word2Vec deployments.
CBOW training signal
One example per position in corpus. Window size 2 → 1 prediction per step. Fast.
Skip-gram training signal
Multiple examples per position. Window size 2 → up to 4 predictions per step. 4× more training signals from same data.
Rare word handling
Poor — rare words get averaged away into common context. Their signal is weak.
Rare word handling
Excellent — each rare word generates many training pairs. The model builds a dedicated rich representation for it.

From count matrices to transformers — the embedding family tree
Every embedding algorithm below is trying to solve the same problem: how do you turn a word into a list of numbers that captures its meaning? Each generation improved on the last.
1986
One-hot vectors
Each word = a vector of zeros with a single 1. "cat" = [0,0,1,0,0…]. Simple but useless — every word is equally distant from every other. No meaning, just identity.
1990s
TF-IDF and co-occurrence matrices
Count how often words appear near each other across a corpus. Build a giant matrix. Words in similar rows are similar. Works but vectors are enormous (one dimension per vocab word) and sparse.
2003
Neural Probabilistic Language Model — Bengio et al.
First neural network to learn dense word embeddings as a byproduct of predicting the next word. Slow but proved the concept: a small dense vector can encode more meaning than a huge sparse one.
2013
Word2Vec — CBOW and Skip-gram (Mikolov et al., Google)
Stripped away everything except the embedding layer. Trained on 100 billion words in under a day on a laptop. Produced king−man+woman≈queen. Launched the modern era of embeddings. Paper: arXiv:1301.3781.
2014
GloVe — Global Vectors (Pennington et al., Stanford)
Instead of local windows, GloVe factorizes the entire corpus co-occurrence matrix globally. Slightly more stable training than Word2Vec. Still used in NLP pipelines today. Paper: Pennington et al. 2014.
2017
FastText — Subword embeddings (Bojanowski et al., Meta)
Word2Vec treats "run", "running", "runner" as totally separate words. FastText breaks words into character n-grams and learns embeddings for each piece. Can represent words it has never seen. Great for morphologically rich languages.
2018
ELMo — Contextual embeddings (Peters et al., Allen AI)
Same word, different vector every time based on context. "Bank" near "river" gets a different vector than "bank" near "loan". Built on bidirectional LSTMs. First widely-used contextual embedding. Paper: arXiv:1802.05365.
2018
BERT — Masked Language Model (Devlin et al., Google)
Deep contextual embeddings using transformers. Masks 15% of tokens and predicts them from context — a deep, bidirectional version of CBOW. 12–24 transformer layers produce embeddings so rich they still power most NLP systems today. Paper: arXiv:1810.04805.
2020+
GPT-style next-token prediction (OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta…)
The embedding layer is now just the first step of a 96-layer transformer. The "embeddings" that matter are the final-layer representations after attention has run — dynamically shaped by every word in the context window. Word2Vec's descendant, scaled 10,000×.
Key paper — arXiv:1301.3781
Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space — Mikolov et al. 2013
The Word2Vec paper that introduced both CBOW and Skip-gram. Showed that a shallow network trained purely to predict words from context learns geometry: gender, country-capital relationships, verb tenses — all as directions in vector space. Trained on Google News corpus (100 billion words). One of the most cited papers in all of computer science. Accessible to read — no heavy math required.
