The same architecture that reads and writes also sees and listens
The big idea
One architecture — three modalities
You have already learned how transformers process words by turning them into tokens and embedding vectors. The same mechanism works for images and audio — you just need to figure out how to convert pixels and sound waves into a sequence of vectors. Once you do that, the transformer takes over exactly as before: attention, feed-forward, repeat.

Vision Transformers — 2 of 10
Turning a photograph into a sentence
An image is worth 16×16 words — Dosovitskiy et al., Google Brain 2020
The core problem
Transformers need sequences — images are grids
A transformer cannot process a 224×224 grid of pixels directly. It needs a sequence of tokens. The key insight of ViT: treat an image like a sentence by dividing it into small rectangular patches, then process those patches exactly like word tokens. The transformer never knows it is looking at an image.
The numbers
196 patches from one photo
A standard 224×224 image divided into 16×16 patches produces exactly 196 patches. Each 16×16×3 patch is flattened to 768 numbers. Compare: a 100-word sentence has ~130 tokens. A ViT reads 196 "visual words" at once — then attention connects them all.

Vision Transformers — 3 of 10
The [CLS] token — one vector to represent the whole image
Borrowed directly from BERT — a learnable summary token
Why a CLS token?
The transformer needs a single summary of the whole image
After 196 patches pass through the transformer, you have 196 output vectors. For image classification you need one answer ("this is a cat"). The solution: prepend a special learnable [CLS] token before the patches. Through attention, this token gathers information from all 196 patches simultaneously. Its final output vector represents the entire image and is fed to the classification head (an MLP).

Positional embeddings
Transformers are order-agnostic, so ViT adds a learned positional embedding to each patch — similar to how text transformers encode word position. Patches learn a 2D grid structure from training: neighbouring positions get similar embeddings automatically.
No convolutions needed
CNNs use sliding filter windows to detect local patterns. ViT skips all of that — pure attention between patches. ViT needs much more data to learn good features, but its global attention lets it model long-range relationships that CNNs struggle with.
Vision Transformers — 4 of 10
ViT vs CNN — different eyes on the same picture
Each architecture has fundamentally different assumptions about what matters in an image
CNN — local first, global later
A CNN slides a small filter (3×3 or 5×5 pixels) across the image, detecting edges, textures, and patterns locally. Later layers combine local features into global ones. Data-efficient — generalises well with less training data. Struggles with long-range relationships across the image.
ViT — global from layer 1
ViT's attention computes relationships between ALL patches from the very first layer. Can immediately connect distant regions. Needs huge amounts of data to learn that neighbouring pixels matter — no built-in locality assumption. Once pretrained at scale, dramatically outperforms CNNs.
Major ViT variants — the family tree
ViT-B/16, ViT-L/16, ViT-H/14 — original 2020 sizes. B=base (86M), L=large (307M), H=huge (632M). The number is the patch size in pixels.

DeiT (2021) — Data-efficient ViT. Uses knowledge distillation from a CNN teacher to train on ImageNet alone (no extra private data). Made ViT practical without Google-scale datasets.

Swin Transformer (2021) — Applies attention within local windows and shifts windows between layers. Hierarchical like a CNN. State of the art on detection and segmentation tasks.

MAE — Masked Autoencoder (2022) — Pretrains ViT by masking 75% of patches and reconstructing them — analogous to BERT's masked language modelling for images.

ViT-22B (2023) — Google's 22 billion parameter ViT. One of the largest vision models ever trained.

Vision + Language — 5 of 10
CLIP — teaching a model that images and words mean the same thing
Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training · OpenAI 2021 · arXiv:2103.00020
The key idea
Train two encoders to agree on what belongs together
CLIP trains a text encoder (transformer) and an image encoder (ViT) simultaneously on 400 million (image, caption) pairs from the internet. The goal: make the image vector and the matching caption vector as similar as possible (high dot product), while pushing non-matching pairs apart. This is called contrastive learning. The result: an image of a cat and the text "a photo of a cat" end up in the same location in embedding space.

Why CLIP matters
CLIP is the text encoder inside Stable Diffusion and DALL-E. When you type a prompt, CLIP converts it to a vector. The diffusion model then generates an image whose ViT embedding is close to that vector. CLIP made text-to-image generation possible at scale.
Zero-shot classification
Give CLIP the text "a photo of a {class}" for 1,000 ImageNet classes. Embed a new image. The class whose text embedding is most similar to the image wins — no fine-tuning required. CLIP achieves 76% accuracy on ImageNet this way, matching ResNet-50 trained specifically on ImageNet.
Learning Transferable Visual Models from Natural Language Supervision (CLIP) — Radford et al., OpenAI 2021 (arXiv:2103.00020). Foundation of all major text-to-image models including Stable Diffusion and DALL-E.
Speech Transformers — 6 of 10
What is sound — and how do computers hear it?
From pressure waves in air to numbers a transformer can process
Sound as a waveform
Recording audio creates a list of numbers — 16,000 per second
A microphone measures air pressure thousands of times per second. At 16,000 Hz (standard for speech), 1 second of audio = 16,000 numbers from −1 to +1. One minute = 960,000 numbers. That is a very long sequence for a transformer to process directly — too long and too noisy.

Why not raw waveforms?
Raw audio at 16 kHz for 30 seconds = 480,000 numbers. Attention is O(N²) — that is 2.3×10¹¹ attention scores. The Mel spectrogram compresses 480,000 samples into 80×3,000 = 240,000 values. A CNN frontend further compresses this to 1,500 time frames, making it tractable.
What is a Mel spectrogram?
A Mel spectrogram shows which frequencies are present in sound over time. "Mel" means the frequency scale is warped to match how human hearing works — we are more sensitive to differences in low frequencies. 80 Mel frequency bins capture the full range of human speech.
Speech Transformers — 7 of 10
Wav2Vec 2.0 — BERT for speech
Self-supervised speech representation learning · Meta AI 2020 · arXiv:2006.11477
The BERT parallel
Mask parts of the audio — predict what was masked
Just like BERT masks 15% of text tokens and predicts them from context, Wav2Vec 2.0 masks parts of the audio and trains the transformer to reconstruct them. The model never sees transcripts — it learns from unlabelled audio only. After pretraining on up to 1,000,000 hours of unlabelled speech, it is fine-tuned on a small labelled dataset to produce a speech recognition system that rivals models trained with hundreds of times more labelled data.

Why it matters
With just 10 minutes of labelled speech, a fine-tuned Wav2Vec 2.0 achieves competitive word error rates. This is transformative for low-resource languages — hundreds of languages have never had enough labelled data for traditional speech recognition. Wav2Vec 2.0 changed that fundamentally.
HuBERT and WavLM
HuBERT (Meta 2021) improves on Wav2Vec 2.0 by clustering audio features offline, then using cluster assignments as pseudo-labels for masked prediction — closer to BERT's actual procedure. WavLM (Microsoft 2022) adds denoising to the objective, making it better for speaker identification and diarisation tasks.
wav2vec 2.0: A Framework for Self-Supervised Learning of Speech Representations — Baevski et al., Meta 2020 (arXiv:2006.11477). ASR with 10 minutes of labelled data. Trained on 1M hours of unlabelled audio.
Speech Transformers — 8 of 10
Whisper — speech recognition for 96 languages
Robust Speech Recognition via Large-Scale Weak Supervision · OpenAI 2022 · arXiv:2212.04356
The key difference from Wav2Vec 2.0
Fully supervised on 680,000 hours — no self-supervision needed
Whisper skips self-supervised pretraining entirely. Instead it trains directly on (audio, transcript) pairs scraped from the internet — 680,000 hours across 96 languages. The model is an encoder-decoder transformer: the encoder reads the Mel spectrogram, the decoder generates text tokens autoregressively. Whisper also performs language detection, voice activity detection, and speech translation — all in one model with special control tokens.

Whisper model sizes
Tiny (39M) · Base (74M) · Small (244M) · Medium (769M) · Large (1.55B). Each roughly doubles layers and heads. Word Error Rate on English clean speech (Large-v3): ~2.7% — near human level. Larger = better accuracy, slower speed.
What Whisper cannot do
Whisper is optimised for linguistic content, not speaker identity. It cannot tell you WHO is speaking (speaker diarisation) — Wav2Vec 2.0 and WavLM are better for that. Whisper also hallucinates on silence or very noisy audio — generating plausible-sounding text even when no speech is present.
Robust Speech Recognition via Large-Scale Weak Supervision (Whisper) — Radford et al., OpenAI 2022 (arXiv:2212.04356). 680K hours, 96 languages, single encoder-decoder model, near-human WER on clean English.
Multimodal — 9 of 10
How modern AI models see, hear, and talk at once
GPT-4o, Gemini, Claude — all modalities as token sequences in one transformer
The unifying insight
Everything becomes tokens — then attention handles the rest
Every modality eventually becomes a sequence of embedding vectors, and the transformer's attention mechanism connects them. Text becomes word tokens. Images become patch tokens (via a ViT encoder). Audio becomes spectrogram frame tokens. Video becomes frame-level patch tokens across time. Once everything is in the same token format, a single transformer can attend across all of them in a single forward pass.

GPT-4o — native multimodal
The "o" means omni. The first major model trained to process text, images, and audio natively in a single unified model — not separate models stitched together. Response time for audio: ~232ms (near real-time conversation). Can see an image and answer a question about it in one forward pass.
The challenge — modality alignment
Training a single model on multiple modalities requires careful data balancing. Too much image data and the model becomes strong at vision but weak at text. Special techniques: modality-specific learning rates, cross-modal attention gates, and carefully curated training ratios to prevent one modality from dominating.
